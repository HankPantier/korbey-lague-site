Most service business owners in Massachusetts chose their entity structure once — probably at startup, probably on the advice of a lawyer or an online formation service — and haven't seriously revisited it since. That single decision, left unexamined, can quietly drain five figures from a growing business every year. The S-corp vs. LLC question isn't a formation checkbox. It's a recurring tax calculation that deserves a fresh look every time your revenue climbs, your payroll changes, or your personal financial picture shifts.

This post breaks down how each structure actually works, where Massachusetts adds its own wrinkle, and why the "right" answer in year two might be the wrong answer in year five.

## What the Structures Actually Mean (Beyond the Buzzwords)

An LLC — a Limited Liability Company — is a legal structure that, by default, is treated as a pass-through entity for federal tax purposes. A single-member LLC is taxed like a sole proprietorship. A multi-member LLC is taxed like a partnership. In both cases, the IRS doesn't tax the business separately; profits flow to the owners' personal returns, and owners pay self-employment tax on the full net profit — 15.3% on the first $168,600 (2024 threshold) and 2.9% above that.

An S-corporation is also a pass-through entity, meaning profits pass to shareholders' personal returns and avoid corporate-level federal income tax. The key mechanical difference: S-corp owners who work in the business are required to pay themselves a "reasonable salary," which is subject to payroll taxes. Profit distributions *above* that salary are not subject to self-employment or FICA taxes. That gap between what you pay yourself and what the business earns is where the S-corp tax savings live.

You can read the IRS's technical framework here: [IRS: S Corporation Overview](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/s-corporations). For a broader structural overview, the [SBA: Choose a Business Structure](https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business/choose-business-structure) guide is also useful as a starting point — though it doesn't account for Massachusetts-specific rules, which matter.

## The Massachusetts Layer

Massachusetts adds complexity that national guides tend to skip. The state imposes a flat 5% personal income tax on pass-through income, which both LLCs and S-corps pay at the owner level. But Massachusetts also has its own S-corp rules worth knowing:

- Massachusetts generally follows the federal S-corp election, but it taxes S-corp income at the entity level for certain built-in gains and passive income situations.
- The state's corporate excise tax applies to S-corps, though it's generally modest — $456 minimum or 0.26% of net worth, whichever is greater.
- For LLCs taxed as partnerships, Massachusetts assesses a separate entity-level fee based on gross receipts, ranging from $500 to $5,000 annually depending on revenue.

For a service business generating $400,000 in annual revenue, that LLC fee alone can hit $1,500 or more before you've even started calculating self-employment tax. It's not catastrophic in isolation, but it factors into the full comparison.

## Where the Savings Actually Come From

Here's the scenario that comes up constantly with [service businesses](/industries/service-businesses): a consultant, agency owner, or healthcare professional who structured as a single-member LLC and is now generating $200,000 or more in net profit. Every dollar of that profit flows to their Schedule SE and gets hit with self-employment tax.

If that same business elects S-corp status, the owner might set a reasonable salary of $90,000 — a figure the IRS requires to be defensible relative to industry norms and the actual role — and take the remaining $110,000 as a distribution. Payroll taxes apply only to the $90,000. The $110,000 distribution avoids FICA entirely.

At a combined 15.3% self-employment tax rate, the savings on $110,000 in distribution income could approach $16,830 in a year. Even after accounting for payroll processing costs (roughly $1,500–$3,000 annually) and additional state compliance, the net savings are substantial and recurring.

The math, simplified:

| Scenario | Net Profit | SE/Payroll Tax Exposure | Estimated Tax | Estimated Annual Savings |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Single-member LLC | $200,000 | 100% of profit | ~$28,000 | — |
| S-Corp (salary: $90K) | $200,000 | Salary only | ~$13,000 | ~$15,000 |

Numbers are illustrative and will vary based on deductions, state rules, and individual circumstances. But the direction is consistent: at higher profit levels, the S-corp structure wins on self-employment tax, often decisively.

## When the LLC Still Wins

The S-corp math doesn't always favor the election. There are real scenarios where staying an LLC is the right call:

**Low net profit.** Below roughly $40,000–$60,000 in net profit, the payroll administration costs of running an S-corp often exceed the tax savings. The break-even varies by situation, but this is a genuine ceiling below which the election frequently doesn't pencil out.

**Multiple owners with complex equity arrangements.** S-corps are limited to 100 shareholders, and — critically — they can only have one class of stock. If your business has investors who want preferred returns, or partners with different economic arrangements, an S-corp election creates structural problems that a partnership-taxed LLC avoids.

**You're about to sell.** The tax treatment of a business sale can differ meaningfully based on entity type. Some buyers prefer asset purchases, which have different implications for S-corps vs. LLCs. Making an S-corp election within five years of a sale can trigger the built-in gains tax. This is a situation where entity structure should be part of your exit conversation, not an afterthought.

**You're in a QBID sweet spot.** The Section 199A qualified business income deduction may treat LLC and S-corp income similarly for many service businesses — but not all. Specified service trades or businesses (SSTBs) face income phase-outs that can affect how the deduction applies. The interaction between 199A and entity type is one of those areas where generalized guidance often gets it wrong.

## Why This Is a Recurring Conversation, Not a One-Time Decision

Revenue milestones change the analysis. A business that rightfully stayed an LLC at $80,000 in profit should probably revisit the question at $150,000 and again at $300,000. Personal circumstances matter too — an owner who adds a spouse to payroll, sells a piece of real estate, or starts drawing from retirement accounts may find that their overall tax picture shifts in ways that change the entity calculus.

Electing S-corp status isn't a unilateral decision, either. There are deadlines, IRS forms, and in some cases late-election relief procedures. The election takes effect prospectively, which means timing matters if you're mid-year.

## What a Proper Entity Analysis Actually Looks Like

A real entity type analysis isn't a Google search or a five-minute conversation. It involves pulling your last two to three years of tax returns, modeling the tax impact of each structure at your current profit level and projected growth, considering your state obligations under Massachusetts law, and stress-testing the structure against near-term changes — a new partner, a planned sale, a shift in the nature of the business.

It also involves making sure the structure you choose gets maintained correctly. An S-corp that doesn't run actual payroll, pay a reasonable salary, or file the right state forms can have its election challenged. The tax savings evaporate quickly if the structure isn't maintained properly.

## Common Questions

**Can I switch from an LLC to an S-corp mid-year?**
Yes, though the timing has rules. The IRS generally requires the S-corp election to be filed by March 15 for it to take effect in the current tax year (for calendar-year businesses). If you miss that deadline, there is a late election relief procedure under Revenue Procedure 2013-30, but it requires a reasonable cause explanation. The cleanest path is to plan the election before the tax year begins.

**Does Massachusetts recognize the S-corp election automatically?**
Massachusetts generally follows the federal S-corp election, but the state has its own corporate excise tax and specific rules that apply. You don't file a separate state S-corp election, but you do need to comply with Massachusetts corporate requirements — including the annual report to the Secretary of State — which differ from what an LLC faces.

**What's a "reasonable salary" for an S-corp owner?**
The IRS requires S-corp owner-employees to pay themselves compensation comparable to what they'd pay an outside hire for the same work. There's no fixed number; it's based on industry, role, hours, and geography. Setting the salary artificially low to maximize distributions is a known audit trigger. A CPA familiar with your industry can help set a defensible number.

**My accountant set me up as an LLC three years ago. Should I just switch now?**
Not necessarily — and not without running the numbers first. The right structure depends on your current profit level, ownership situation, future plans, and Massachusetts-specific factors. The conversion itself has steps and costs. But if your net profit has grown significantly since formation, it's absolutely worth a dedicated analysis to find out what you're leaving on the table.
