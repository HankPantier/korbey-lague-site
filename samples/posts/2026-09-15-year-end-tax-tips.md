December always feels too late, but most year-end tax moves only need to be made by the 31st. Here are six worth running through with your CPA — most take half an hour, all of them are reversible if you change your mind.

## 1. Time your year-end equipment purchases

If you've been eyeing a piece of equipment or software, putting the purchase order in before December 31 means you can section-179 it against this year's income. The catch: the item has to be *in service* by the 31st, not just paid for. A laptop that's still in shipping doesn't count.

## 2. Make your final 401(k) contribution

Solo 401(k) contributions need to be elected by December 31 even if the dollars don't land until your tax filing deadline. SEP-IRAs are more forgiving — those can be funded any time before you file.

## 3. Defer income (when it makes sense)

If you're below the next tax bracket and your January will be busy anyway, defer December invoices into January. If you're *above* the next bracket this year and expect lower income next year, the opposite move is right.

## 4. Bunch your charitable giving

Two years of donations made in one year (typically December) lets you itemize one year and take the standard deduction the next. If you donate appreciated stock, you skip the capital gains *and* take the full market-value deduction — a small but real win.

## 5. Review your accountable plan

If you reimburse employees for home-office costs, vehicle use, or supplies, year-end is the time to make sure those reimbursements are running through a documented accountable plan. Otherwise they show up as taxable wages.

## 6. Set next year's quarterly estimates

Underpayment penalties are small but irritating. A 30-minute conversation in late December — based on this year's actuals and next year's expectations — usually catches the biggest surprises.
